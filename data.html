We have begun construction of the data base by consulting local experts in various localities, collaborating with colleagues elsewhere who have worked with similar data (a shoutout to our friends at mappingthegayguides.org!), and extracting data from LGBTQ+ travel guides published from the 1960s through the mid-2010s. We recognize that the accuracy of this sort of information, from these sorts of sources, will always be both uncertain and contested. For this reason we try to "ground truth" as much of the data as possible (using a variety of methods) and have included an indication for each piece of data as to how accurate we believe it to be. Your contributions to this process will help immensely with that process!

In keeping with the principles of open and accessible scholarship, we are making our compiled data available to everyone. To ensure compliance with our terms and policy, please take a moment to read and accept them before downloading the data.

By accessing this data, I affirm that I will use it solely for lawful and ethical purposes. I understand that any unauthorized or malicious use of the data, such as harassment of individuals, organizations, or businesses associated with the entries, is strictly prohibited. The data is intended for research or personal use only, and commercial usage is not permitted. I acknowledge that any violation of these terms may result in legal consequences.

1 The reason there are two systems of classification is that historically the LGBTQ+ community has faced countless discrimination using obscure or coded language to communicate was a way of combating social stigma. Our data sources too made use of coded language for their listings. However, as the meanings of some of these symbols and codes changed over time, they can be very inconsistent from year to year, this is especially true since earlier publications sometimes contained degoratory/discriminatory phrasings by today's standards. We have developed a modern classification system using some of those code words as the basis, which better reflects our values. However the old codes are kept as part of the dataset for those who may have use for it.
